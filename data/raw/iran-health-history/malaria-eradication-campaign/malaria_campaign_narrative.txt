Iran Malaria Control & Eradication Campaign — narrative timeline with embedded statistics
Compiled 2026-07-13 from open-web secondary sources (WHO EMRO, WHO results-report country
story, and academic/trade-press summaries of the malaria historiography). All figures below
are transcribed as stated in the citing source; no case-count figure was interpolated,
estimated, or back-calculated by this agent.

1945 — First malaria-training course for anti-malaria campaign personnel established in Iran,
laying institutional groundwork ahead of the country's first pesticide-based control effort.
[Source: WHO EMRO malaria strategy/policy page, via WebSearch synthesis]

1947 — DDT used for the first time in mosquito vector control in Iran, in a pilot study
targeting malaria-hyperendemic villages near Tehran. This predates Iran's formal national
eradication programme by a decade and mirrors the broader WHO-led global DDT-spraying wave of
the late 1940s.
[Source: WHO EMRO / academic summary, via WebSearch synthesis]

1948-1956 — A sustained anti-malarial campaign combining drug prophylaxis and treatment,
indoor residual spraying with DDT, and anti-larval control measures ran across this period and
"considerably decreased malaria infection rate in most endemic areas" per the cited summary.
No annual case-count figures for this specific window were located in this pass (a genuine
gap, not omitted for lack of effort — the primary source likely to have this, the WHO/Global
Fund-published "History of the successful struggle against malaria in the Islamic Republic of
Iran" report, could not be fetched: its landing page at iran.un.org returned only a publication
abstract, not the underlying PDF content, via the WebFetch tool).
[Source: academic/WHO summary literature, via WebSearch synthesis]

1957 — Iran's national Malaria Eradication Programme (MEP) formally launched, joining the WHO's
1955-declared Global Malaria Eradication Programme. This is the flagship, most-cited start date
for Iran's modern anti-malaria effort in the secondary literature.
[Source: multiple academic sources via WebSearch synthesis, cross-confirmed across at least
three independent search results]

~1958-1960s ("as recently as sixty years ago" per a 2018-dated source, implying a reference
point of roughly the late 1950s) — Iran was recording an estimated FIVE MILLION malaria cases
per year at the pre-eradication-programme peak burden. This is the single most striking
quantitative anchor located for this campaign and is presented as an order-of-magnitude
estimate, not a precise annual figure with a specific year attached — the source states "sixty
years ago" relative to its own ~2018 publication date, which is treated here as pointing to the
late-1950s/early-1960s period just before or at the start of the MEP's main effect, consistent
with the historiography that credits 1957-1980 as the main eradication-effort window.
[Source: end malaria.org / Global Fund "Iran Story" summary via WebSearch synthesis]

1957-1980 — Indoor residual spraying with DDT, larviciding, and mass drug administration
"substantially reduced malaria incidence by the 1970s" and by 1980 the programme had "almost
interrupted malaria transmission in the north parts of the country." This is consistent with
Iran being widely cited internationally (alongside Sri Lanka and India, whose specific
comparator figures are: India ~110 million cases in 1955 down to under 1 million reported by
1968; Sri Lanka ~2.8 million cases in 1946 down to 18 reported cases by 1966) as one of the
Global Malaria Eradication Programme's qualified successes for this window, though — as with
most GMEP target countries — full eradication was not sustained.
[Source: WHO / academic summary via WebSearch synthesis]

1980 — WHO's global objectives shifted from "eradication" to "control" as the original 1955
GMEP was formally abandoned worldwide (multiple technical and resource obstacles, comparable to
outcomes in most GMEP target countries outside a handful of island/peninsula successes like Sri
Lanka and Taiwan). Iran's programme followed this same global shift in framing.
[Source: WHO / academic summary via WebSearch synthesis]

1990 — By this point, Iran's residual malaria burden had become geographically concentrated in
just THREE provinces in the southeast of the country (unnamed in the source, but consistent
with Sistan-Baluchestan, Hormozgan, and Kerman being the well-documented residual-malaria belt
in later Iranian public-health literature, given Afghanistan/Pakistan border proximity and
vector ecology) — down from a nationwide burden of an estimated 5 million cases/year three
decades earlier.
[Source: academic summary via WebSearch synthesis]
