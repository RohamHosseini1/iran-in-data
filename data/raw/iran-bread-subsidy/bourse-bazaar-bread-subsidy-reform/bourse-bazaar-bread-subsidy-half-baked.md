## Summary of content

The article argues that what is commonly called Iran's "bread subsidy reform" (implemented from
May 2022) was not actually a direct subsidy on bread, but the elimination of a preferential
foreign-exchange rate that had indirectly subsidized wheat and other staple-good importers.

### Key statistics

- The subsidized/preferential exchange rate had been fixed at **IRR 42,000 per US dollar since
  2019**, versus a "budget rate" of roughly **IRR 230,000 per US dollar** as of March 2022.
- Iran had been allocating approximately **$12 billion annually** in hard currency on this
  subsidized basis in recent years.
- The list of goods eligible for the subsidized exchange rate had already been narrowed from
  **25 items to 7 goods** (wheat, corn, barley, oilseeds, edible oil, soybeans, medical goods) by
  September 2021, ahead of the full reform.
- Bread and cereal prices had **quadrupled since 2018**, according to the article, even before the
  May 2022 reform took full effect.

### Main arguments

1. **Mischaracterized as a "bread subsidy" cut.** The reform eliminated a currency-exchange
   subsidy benefiting importers, not a subsidy paid directly to consumers or bakeries.
2. **Four distortionary effects of the old system** identified by the article:
   - Poor targeting — could not respond to domestic harvest shortfalls or global wheat price
     spikes.
   - Fiscal deficit exacerbation — a major driver of inflation; the Central Bank of Iran faced
     "increasing difficulty in meeting demand" for the subsidized currency.
   - Exchange-rate volatility — diverted resources away from stabilizing the broader foreign
     exchange market.
   - Subject to abuse — politically connected importers reportedly received preferential
     allocations, with profiteering common.
3. **Reform was long-delayed.** The Iran Chamber of Commerce had called for eliminating the
   subsidized rate as early as 2019, but the Rouhani administration postponed action after the
   2017–2018 protests.
4. **Practical workarounds already existed.** Many importers had already shifted to the NIMA
   foreign-exchange market (rather than the official subsidized rate) because it offered faster
   payment processing.
5. **Mitigation measures announced:** two months of targeted cash transfers and electronic coupons
   aimed at vulnerable populations to offset the price shock.
