## Summary of content

The article examines how the Raisi administration frames Iran's 2022 subsidy-reform program
(covering bread/flour, other staples, and historically energy) — contrasting a religious framing
("economic jihad," tied to the Shia concept of *entezar*, waiting for the return of the Twelfth
Imam/Mahdi) against the substance of the policy, which the author argues resembles conventional
market-liberalization measures (subsidy cuts, tax increases) similar to IMF-style recommendations.

### Key statistics

**Energy subsidies (context/comparison for the broader subsidy system):**
- Annual cost to the Iranian state: approximately **$100 billion per year**.
- As of a 2010 baseline, energy subsidies represented roughly **25% of GDP**.
- Energy subsidies comprised about **one-third of total government subsidies**.

**2022 cash-assistance program (replacing/offsetting subsidy cuts):**
- **30%** of Iranians were slated to receive approximately **$15/month**.
- **60%** were slated to receive approximately **$10/month**.
- The remaining **10%** (higher-income households) were to receive no cash payments.

**Wheat/bread subsidies specifically:**
- Iran consumes approximately **8.5 million tons of wheat annually**.
- The government had been subsidizing roughly **10 million tons per year** (i.e., subsidizing more
  than is actually consumed domestically).
- An estimated **30%** of subsidized wheat was reportedly diverted to smuggling or unauthorized use
  in confectionary/other production.
- A new tracking system was projected to save approximately **$1.5 billion annually** by curbing
  this diversion.

**Inflation / poverty context (mid-2022):**
- National average inflation in June 2022: **52.5%**.
- Rural-area inflation: **58.2%**.
- Poverty rate: over **one-third of the population** affected.

### Main arguments

The article contends that the Raisi government uses religious-ideological framing ("economic
jihad," the theme of waiting/*entezar*) to justify short-term economic hardship from subsidy
reform, while the underlying policy substance — cutting subsidies, raising some taxes — closely
tracks conventional market-liberalization prescriptions of the kind associated with IMF
recommendations, rather than being a uniquely ideological/revolutionary economic program.
