Extension and Development Corps (EDCs) — establishment and structure:
The Extension and Development Corps (EDCs) were created in 1964 through an Imperial Government bill modeled on military lines, drawing human resources from high school and university graduates in agriculture, architecture, economics, poly-technical arts, mechanization, electrical and civil engineering, physics, civics, social sciences, and veterinary fields. Training commenced 1 April 1964 at the Academy of Military Sciences. Operations continued until the 1979 Revolution, when they were disbanded as the Iran-Iraq War commenced.

Corpsmen composition and service terms:
- University graduates received second-lieutenant rank.
- High-school graduates received sergeant rank (titled "technicians").
- Required service: 18 months total (4-month training course + 14 months residential service in rural districts).

Operational impact:
- EDC interventions resulted in the use of 90,000 tons of fertilizer by farmers in 1965 (early fertilizer-adoption indicator).
- The program stimulated soil-testing applications significantly (no numeric figure given).
- Corpsmen were equipped with sprayers, vehicles, extension kits, improved seeds, and printed materials covering agricultural, livestock, and horticultural activities.

Modern-era comparison figures (NOT White-Revolution era — included in source for historical contrast, kept here for completeness but out of scope for pre-1990 gap):
- As of a 2003 country paper (Fami, 2003): approximately 6,497 professional extension staff nationally, of which 7 percent were women; approximately 37,326 local extension agents, of which 6.4 percent were women.

Source citations given in the GFRAS page: papers presented at the 1967 CENTO (Central Treaty Organization) Conference on Agricultural Extension, authored by Jaffari, P. and Rassi, J.; and a 2003 Iran country paper by Fami.
