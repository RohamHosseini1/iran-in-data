BEHDĀRĪ (maintaining health), term applied in Persian usage to the entire organization and services provided either by government or by various other agencies to secure the health of the people.

At the outset of Reżā Shah's reign (1299-1305 Š./1920-26): service infrastructure consisted of 21 hospitals (906 total beds, including 10 foreign-missionary hospitals with 650 beds); 90 urban dispensaries; 11 quarantine stations. Total physicians (Iranian + foreign): 945 (293 graduates + 652 certified doctors). Domestic medical education: 1 college of medicine, 1 school of pharmacy, 1 school of midwifery (all offshoots of the Tehran Polytechnic). Health could not cope with the needs of ~10 million people (Gilmour, p. 14). Malaria caused 30-40 percent of all illness; >57 percent of reported Tehran deaths were from preventable infectious disease, nearly half in children under 4. Health budget for FY 1299 Š./1920: estimated at 9 million rials (total government expenditure that year: Rls 228 million / 5.4 million sterling pounds; oil revenue: Rls 32 million / 800,000 sterling pounds).

Institutional timeline: State Health Council est. 1904; General Department of Health est. 1926 (Ministry of Interior); Ministry of Health (Wezārat-e Behdārī) formed 1941; Public Health Cooperative Organization est. 1952 (with US Point Four Program), fully integrated into Ministry of Health 1962; Ministry of Health and Welfare created 1976.

HEALTH CORPS (Sepāh-e Behdāšt): formed in 1343 Š./1964 -- young volunteers who went to rural areas to work on rudimentary health and sanitation projects as part of their military service. Mobile and semi-mobile medical units were sent throughout the country under the auspices of the Ministry of Health (General Department of Health Corps) and the Iranian armed forces.

By 1357 Š./mid-1978: total health care workers and facilities estimated at 79,710 (detailed in source Tables 1 and 2, which are embedded as print plates/images on the original page and were not extractable as machine-readable data via this retrieval -- flagged as a gap). Also present: 2 leprosariums, 1 leprosy rehabilitation village, several "health houses" in various provinces.

Medical education growth: from 1 college of medicine (1850, Tehran Polytechnic) to, by 1357 Š./1978: 18 medical colleges, 7 post-graduate specialty training centers, 1 school of public health, 32 nursing schools, 90 assistant-nurse training schools, 11 midwifery schools, 60 health-technologist schools, 5 rural-health-worker training centers, 5 pharmacy schools, 5 dentistry schools, 4 veterinary schools, 8 technician-training centers.

Immunization: in 1302 Š./1923, smallpox vaccination had no meaningful coverage; by 1355 Š./1976, annual immunization coverage reached 5 million children.

HEALTH EXPENDITURE TIME SERIES (prose-stated, from source Table 4 description):
- FY 1299 Š./1920: total government expenditure Rls 228 million (5.4M pounds sterling); health expenditure Rls 900,000 (22,500 pounds sterling); oil revenue Rls 32 million (800,000 pounds sterling).
- FY 1327-53 Š./1948-1974: health-care expenditure grew from Rls 1.5 billion to Rls 116.5 billion ($22 million to $166 million).
- FY 1974 (single year): total government expenditure Rls 1,089 billion ($15.5 billion); total health expenditure Rls 35 billion ($0.5 billion); oil revenue Rls 445 billion ($6.5 billion).
- These represent increases over 1920 of: government expenditure x47,700; health expenditure x38,800; oil revenue x14,000 (nominal). CPI for the same period rose ~45x, so real increases were approximately x1,060 (govt), x864 (health), x311 (oil revenue).
- Despite growth, health sector received only 3.2-3.5 percent of total government budget vs an "expected" 6.5 percent (per source's own framing).

Health legislation: 160+ laws/decrees/regulations related to health care approved 1926-1976 (Sotūda).
